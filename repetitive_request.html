The Problem

Every in-house legal team has the same hidden problem.

It's not the complex legal work. It's the same five questions arriving from a hundred different people, every quarter. Repetitive requests are the volume your team can't see, can't measure, and can't escape.

Cost #1

Lawyer time evaporates into 15-minute increments

A 5-attorney team that answers 200 repetitive questions a quarter loses about 50 hours to context-switching. That's a senior associate's entire month of focus time, gone to questions that didn't need their judgment.

Cost #2

Nobody can see the actual workload

Without intake, repetitive requests don't show up in metrics. They live in email threads, hallway conversations, and Slack DMs. The GC can't report on the work that's quietly consuming the team's capacity.

Cost #3

The business stops asking, and starts guessing

When legal feels slow or hard to reach, business owners route around them. Marketing approves its own claim. Sales redlines its own contract. The shortcut creates risk that surfaces months later.

The Wrong Fix

Faster lawyers won't fix this.
The fix is upstream.

Most legal tech today bets on speed. Faster drafting. Smarter summaries. Better redlines. Useful work, but it has a ceiling. The bottleneck isn't how quickly a lawyer answers a question. The bottleneck is that the question reached a lawyer at all.

The biggest gains in any inbound-heavy team have never come from making the expert faster. They've come from controlling what reaches the expert. The ER learned it with triage. Customer support learned it with deflection. In-house legal is the next discipline that learns it.

Common Questions

What in-house legal teams ask before they buy.

Won't the AI assistant get it wrong?+
The assistant is grounded in your policies, playbooks, and prior answers. Not the open internet. It cites sources for every response, escalates anything outside its confidence threshold, and only handles request types your legal team has explicitly approved. Anything novel still reaches a lawyer.
Doesn't deflection just push work to the business?+
No. The goal is to push the answer to the business, not the work. A marketing manager who asks "can I say this?" today is already doing the work of waiting, asking, following up. Self-service gives them the answer faster, on policy, with a defensible record.
Our team is already busy. How long does setup take?+
First workflows typically go live within 4 to 6 weeks. Setup is configuration, not development. Your legal ops team builds with the Checkbox no-code platform. You don't need IT, and you don't need to redesign your processes before you start.
How is this different from a CLM?+
A CLM stores executed contracts. Checkbox handles the layer in front of it. The intake, the triage, the workflow that determines whether a contract even needs to be drafted, and what gets routed to your CLM if so. The two are complementary.
What happens to the requests that still need a lawyer?+
They show up in your team's queue with structured context attached. Who asked, what type, what risk tier, what SLA. Cleaner intake means faster triage and better decisions on what to prioritize.
How do we measure the impact?+
Real-time dashboards show request volume by type, deflection rate, average resolution time, and attorney workload distribution. The data your team needs to report to the GC or the board on legal capacity.
